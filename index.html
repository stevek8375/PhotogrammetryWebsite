Why I Build

Throughout my career in games and film, I was fortunate to work on productions that demanded both artistic creativity and technical problem solving. While animation was my profession, I've always been motivated by something broader: curiosity.

Outside of work, landscape photography became a personal passion. Exploring remote places eventually introduced me to prehistoric rock art, where I encountered a challenge that traditional photography couldn't fully solve. A single photograph captured only one viewpoint, but I wanted to preserve an entire place—its shape, texture, scale, and atmosphere.

That curiosity led me to photogrammetry.

Once I could reconstruct a site digitally, another question emerged:

"What else could I do with it?"

The answer led me into real-time graphics, virtual reality, augmented reality, and interactive visualization. Each project became an opportunity to learn a new technology in order to solve a new problem, whether improving accessibility, preserving fragile cultural heritage, or helping people better understand complex three-dimensional spaces.

Painted Rock in VR

This project demonstrates a complete digital preservation workflow for Painted Rock, one of California's most significant rock art sites. Beginning with field photography and photogrammetry, the project reconstructs the site as an optimized real-time model before presenting it as an immersive virtual reality experience that allows visitors to compare the artwork's current condition with a historically accurate digital restoration.

Hueco Tanks in AR

Hueco Tanks State Park contains one of the largest collections of prehistoric rock art in North America, yet many of these remarkable sites remain inaccessible to the general public. Some pictographs can only be visited on guided tours, while others require hiking over uneven terrain that may not be suitable for visitors with limited mobility.

Inspired by conversations with park rangers, I developed this augmented reality application to make these culturally significant sites more accessible. Using photogrammetry, visitors can examine highly detailed digital replicas directly on their mobile devices, explore them from any angle, and activate enhancement modes that reveal weathered pictographs that are often difficult to distinguish with the naked eye.

The project explores how AR technology can expand access to cultural heritage while complementing—not replacing—the experience of visiting these fragile archaeological sites in person.

Interactive Climbing Route Explorer

Troubleshooting a climbing route traditionally relies on guidebooks, photographs, or videos that illustrate the general path up a rock face. While these resources are helpful, they often make it difficult to understand the three-dimensional relationship between holds or to visualize body positioning from different perspectives.

This prototype explores how photogrammetry and augmented reality can provide climbers with a more intuitive way to study routes before leaving the ground. By creating an accurate 3D model of the climbing area, users can freely examine the route from any viewpoint, identify handholds and footholds, and better understand the sequence of movement through the climb.

Although developed as a proof of concept, the project demonstrates how interactive visualization can transform static reference material into an engaging educational tool for outdoor recreation and sports.
